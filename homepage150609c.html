Aims

To show how the global importance of microbes can be explained by an understanding of their genes, genomes, metabolic diversity, ecology, and evolution.

Learning outcomes

At the end of the module you should be able to:

  • Explain how microbes control gene expression and adapt to their environment (LO1).
  • Discuss the metabolic diversity and ecology of microbes, and their roles in biogeochemical cycles (LO2).
  • Explain modern concepts in microbial evolution and systematics (LO3).
  • Understand and apply modern laboratory techniques for the study of microbes and communicate the results of investigations effectively (LO4).
  • Formulate and discuss an individually selected microbiological research topic (LO5).

Outline of syllabus

Bacteria, archaea and viruses constitute over half of the biomass of life on Earth. This module explores the tremendous diversity and adaptability of microbial life, showing how exciting recent discoveries have led to new understanding of the importance of microbes in marine, freshwater and terrestrial environments and in biogeochemical cycles and global processes. Such knowledge is vital at a time of rapid global change.

In Term 1 lectures, you will learn about the organization of microbial genes and genomes and how microbes regulate the expression of their genes and respond to changes in environmental and nutritional conditions through sensing, signal transduction and transcriptional control. The importance of these mechanisms will be illustrated through important examples of microbial growth, behaviour, development, survival, and interactions with other organisms.

You will learn how the discovery of phenomena such as quorum sensing and biofilm formation requires us to reconsider the nature of bacteria and archaea. Did you know that these “simple” organisms sometimes show social behaviour and act as multicellular entities?

In Term 2, you will learn about the metabolic diversity and physiology of bacterial growth under different conditions. You will see how our understanding of the ecological role of microbes – in biogeochemical cycling, biodegradation and biodeterioration – has been revolutionised by the development of metagenomics and related molecular techniques.

Did you know that the oxygen in every fifth breath you take is made by marine bacteria, or that microbes are alive and active in rocks many kilometres below the surface?

Practical work is an essential feature of microbiology and this module features an exciting programme of work, in which you will devise the right conditions isolating particular types of microbe, extract their DNA and identify these organisms using cutting edge methods of genome sequencing and bioinformatics analysis.
